Swami Vivekananda

1863 - 1902

Patriot Saint of India

Swami Vivekananda (1863-1902) was a prominent Indian Hindu monk and a key figure in the introduction of Indian philosophies of Vedanta and Yoga to the Western world. Born as Narendranath Datta in Kolkata, India, he became a disciple of the mystic Ramakrishna Paramahamsa. Vivekananda is best known for his speech at the Parliament of the World's Religions in Chicago in 1893, where he began with the words "Sisters and brothers of America..." and received a standing ovation for his message of religious tolerance and universal acceptance. He emphasized the importance of selflessness, the unity of all religions, and the potential divinity within every human being. He founded the Ramakrishna Mission and the Ramakrishna Math, which focus on spiritual teachings, education, and social reform. His teachings continue to inspire millions around the world.

Biographies

  • Swami Vivekananda was born as Narendranath Datta on January 12, 1863, in Kolkata, India.
  • He met his spiritual guru, Sri Ramakrishna, in 1881, which led to his deep spiritual awakening.
  • After Ramakrishna's death, he and his fellow disciples took monastic vows and dedicated their lives to spirituality.
  • In 1897, he founded the Ramakrishna Mission for spreading Vedanta teachings and social service.
  • His speech at the 1893 Parliament of the World's Religions in Chicago made him internationally famous.
  • He traveled and lectured extensively in the West, promoting spirituality and Vedanta.
  • Swami Vivekananda passed away on July 4, 1902, leaving a lasting impact on global spirituality and Indian nationalism.
